The Enron scandal was a massive corporate fraud that led to the bankruptcy of Enron Corp. on December 2, 2001. Before its collapse, Enron was the seventh-largest corporation in the United States, an Oregon-incorporated company headquartered in Houston, Texas, engaged in the purchase and sale of natural gas and power, construction and ownership of pipelines, provision of telecommunications services, and energy trading.

The fraud was carried out through a wide-ranging scheme to deceive the investing public about the true performance of Enron's businesses by manipulating publicly reported financial results and making false and misleading public statements. The conspiracy's objectives included reporting recurring earnings that falsely appeared to grow 15-20% annually, concealing large losses, masking the true magnitude of debt, deceiving credit rating agencies, and artificially inflating Enron's stock price.

Background

Kenneth Lee Lay was named Chief Executive Officer and Chairman of the Board of Directors of Enron upon its formation in 1986. He held both positions until February 2001, when he stepped down as CEO but continued as Chairman. After Skilling's abrupt resignation on August 14, 2001, Lay resumed the CEO position and took control of the conspiracy.

Role in the Fraud

According to the indictment, Lay and his co-conspirators engaged in a wide-ranging scheme to deceive the investing public by manipulating Enron's publicly reported financial results and making false and misleading public statements about Enron's financial condition. After Skilling's departure, Lay intensified oversight of day-to-day operations and took over leadership of the conspiracy.

  • Knew of at least $7 billion in embedded losses by September 2001
  • Artificially capped Q3 2001 losses at ~$1 billion to protect credit rating
  • Made false statements to employees, analysts, and credit rating agencies
  • Told employees on September 26, 2001 that Enron stock was "an incredible bargain" while secretly selling $24 million in stock
  • Circumvented goodwill impairment accounting standards regarding Wessex (~$700M)
  • Personally profited ~$300M from Enron stock options and restricted stock (1998-2001), netting over $217M

Bank Fraud (Counts 38-41)

Lay was separately charged with bank fraud for defrauding Bank of America, Chase Bank of Texas, and Compass Bank. Between January 1999 and November 2001, he controlled non-purpose lines of credit totaling up to $50 million but used the proceeds to purchase and carry margin stock, in violation of his representations to the banks.

"[O]ur liquidity is fine. As a matter of fact, it's better than fine, it's strong."
— Kenneth Lay, All Employees Meeting, October 23, 2001 (one month before bankruptcy)
"[W]e don't have anything we're trying to hide. . . . I'm disclosing everything that we've found."
— Kenneth Lay, Analyst Conference Call, November 12, 2001

Trial & Outcome

Found guilty on May 25, 2006 on 10 counts including conspiracy, securities fraud, wire fraud, and bank fraud. Lay died of a heart attack on July 5, 2006, before sentencing. His conviction was subsequently vacated by the court due to his death.

Jeffrey K. Skilling President & CEO

Background

Jeffrey Keith Skilling was employed by or acted as a consultant to Enron from the late 1980s. Enron hired him in August 1990. He held various executive positions, was promoted to President and COO in January 1997, and became President and CEO in February 2001. He reported directly to Lay and resigned abruptly on August 14, 2001, citing "personal reasons."

Role in the Fraud

Skilling spearheaded the conspiracy until his sudden departure in August 2001. He and Causey set annual and quarterly earnings and cash flow targets ("budget targets") for the company and each business unit, then used various "levers" to achieve them when actual results fell short—filling gaps that were at times in the hundreds of millions of dollars.

  • Set and monitored budget targets, manufacturing earnings through fraudulent means when targets weren't met
  • Approved creation of LJM partnerships and Raptor hedging structures with Fastow
  • Made knowingly false statements about EBS, valuing it at $30 billion at January 2000 analyst conference when it had neither the broadband network nor software claimed
  • Concealed massive losses at EES and EBS through fraudulent business segment reporting
  • Approved "Project Grayhawk" to manufacture $85 million in false earnings
  • Fraudulently reserved over $1 billion in trading profits in "Schedule C" reserves
  • Filed false 10-K and 10-Q reports with the SEC and signed false representation letters to auditors

Insider Trading (Counts 42-51)

While in possession of material non-public information, Skilling sold shares of Enron stock generating total proceeds of $62,626,401.90. Between 1998 and 2001, he received approximately $200 million from the sale of Enron stock options and restricted stock, netting over $89 million in profit, and was paid more than $14 million in salary and bonuses.

"Enron's business is in great shape" and "I know this is a bad stock market but Enron's in good shape."
— Jeffrey Skilling, Special Analyst Conference Call, March 23, 2001 (while EBS and EES were failing)

Trial & Outcome

Found guilty on May 25, 2006 on 19 of 28 counts, including conspiracy, securities fraud, insider trading, and making false statements to auditors. Sentenced to 24 years and 4 months in federal prison on October 23, 2006. His sentence was later reduced to 14 years in a 2013 resentencing agreement. Released from custody in February 2019.

Background

Richard A. Causey was a certified public accountant. From 1986 to 1991, he was employed by Arthur Andersen LLP and provided audit services to Enron as its outside auditor. In 1991, Enron hired Causey as Assistant Controller of Enron Gas Services Group. From 1992 to 1997, he served in various executive positions. In 1998, Causey became Enron's Chief Accounting Officer.

Role in the Fraud

As CAO, Causey managed Enron's accounting practices and reported to Lay and Skilling. Together with the CFO Andrew Fastow and Treasurer Ben Glisan, Causey was a principal manager of Enron's finances. He routinely participated in discussions with securities analysts and signed Enron's annual and quarterly SEC filings.

  • Managed the creation and execution of SPEs and Raptor hedging structures
  • Signed the "Global Galactic" agreement with Fastow, memorializing secret side-deals
  • Fraudulently increased asset valuations (e.g., Mariner Energy by $100M) to meet earnings targets
  • Circumvented FAS 125/140 accounting standards for financial asset sales
  • Concealed EES failures by manipulating business segment reporting
  • Shifted EBS costs to other books, changed depreciable life of assets, and halved bonus accruals to hide losses
  • Signed false quarterly and annual representation letters to Enron's auditors

Money Laundering (Counts 7-11)

Causey was separately charged with money laundering conspiracy and substantive money laundering counts related to the Raptor transactions, involving wire transfers totaling millions of dollars among Enron, LJM, Talon, and Timberwolf entities.

Trial & Outcome

Causey pled guilty to securities fraud on December 28, 2005, just before the trial was set to begin. He was sentenced to 66 months (5.5 years) in federal prison. His cooperation was reportedly a significant factor in the government's case against Lay and Skilling.

Background & Role in the Fraud

Andrew Stuart Fastow served as Enron's Chief Financial Officer and supervised structured finance, cash flow, and debt management activities. He was the principal architect of the off-balance-sheet partnerships that concealed Enron's true financial condition.

  • Created and served as managing partner of LJM Cayman (LJM1 and LJM2), the investment partnerships that were purportedly independent third parties but were in fact controlled by Fastow
  • Exploited his dual role as Enron's CFO and LJM's general partner to ensure LJM acted as Enron's surrogate rather than an independent entity
  • Entered into the secret "Global Galactic" agreement with Causey memorializing illegal side-deals
  • Negotiated secret oral side-deals guaranteeing LJM against equity risk in transactions with Enron
  • Personally profited tens of millions of dollars from the LJM arrangements
"Certainly what Andy Fastow did they called 'criminal,' and rightfully so."
— Kenneth Lay, during cross-examination at trial, 2006

Legal Outcome

Fastow pled guilty to two counts of conspiracy in January 2004. He became the government's key cooperating witness and testified extensively at the Lay-Skilling trial. Originally facing a potential 10-year sentence, he was sentenced to 6 years in federal prison in September 2006. Released in 2011.

Role in the Fraud

Ben Glisan served as Enron's Treasurer and assisted in supervising Enron's structured finance, cash flow, and debt management activities. He was identified in the indictment as one of the key conspirators who worked with Lay, Skilling, Causey, and Fastow.

Glisan became the first Enron executive to be sent to prison when he pled guilty to a single count of conspiracy to commit wire and securities fraud in September 2003. He was sentenced to 5 years and was immediately taken into custody, an unusual step that sent a strong signal to other potential cooperators.

Role in the Fraud

David Delainey was the CEO of two major Enron business units: Enron North America (ENA) and Enron Energy Services (EES). He supervised large portions of Enron's wholesale energy business and later its retail energy business. The indictment identifies Delainey as one of Lay, Skilling, and Causey's numerous conspirators.

During the trial, Kenneth Lay testified that Delainey had discussed a critical employee email (the "Ceconi email") with him that described hiding losses and fraudulent contracts at EES. Delainey pled guilty to insider trading and cooperated with prosecutors, testifying at trial about the fraudulent concealment of losses at EES. He was sentenced to 30 months.

Role in the Fraud

Michael Kopper was a Managing Director in Enron's Global Finance group who conducted structured finance activities for Enron and assisted in running important Enron off-balance sheet vehicles. He became the first Enron executive to plead guilty in August 2002.

Kopper ran several of the off-balance-sheet entities that were central to the fraud. Kenneth Lay referenced during his trial testimony that Fastow had claimed to have sold his interest in LJM to Kopper, which Lay said had been one of the "lies" that came to light. Kopper cooperated extensively with prosecutors and was sentenced to 37 months in prison.

Sherron Watkins VP, Corporate Development
SW
TitleVice President, Corporate Development
RoleWhistleblower

Role in the Case

Sherron Watkins was a Vice President at Enron who became the case's most prominent whistleblower. In August 2001, shortly after Skilling's resignation, Watkins wrote a memo to Kenneth Lay warning that the company "could implode in a wave of accounting scandals."

During Kenneth Lay's cross-examination at trial, government prosecutor John Hueston established that Lay knew Ms. Watkins had warned him back in August that Enron could implode, as well as other specific notifications she gave him. Lay acknowledged that an investigation was conducted but claimed it found the accounting to be appropriate.

"And, sir, when you said that, 'I could not have ever contemplated those events,' you knew that Ms. Watkins had warned you back in August that Enron could implode in a wave of accounting scandals, as well as other specific notification she gave you, right, sir?"

"Yes. And, of course, we had an investigation of that."
— Exchange between prosecutor John Hueston and Kenneth Lay, trial transcript, May 2, 2006

Watkins was never charged with any crime. She was later named one of Time magazine's "Persons of the Year" for 2002 alongside two other corporate whistleblowers.

Christopher B. Barry Expert Witness — Defense

ExpertiseFinance & Financial Economics

Background

Christopher B. Barry is a Professor of Finance at Texas Christian University (TCU), holding the Robert and Maria Lowdon Chair of Business Administration. He received his doctorate from Indiana University in 1973 and published approximately 38-39 articles in leading finance journals. He founded centers for the study of financial institutions at both SMU and the University of Florida.

Testimony

Barry was called as an expert defense witness for Kenneth Lay in the 2006 trial. He had previously worked on the Enron civil litigation starting in March/April 2002 and was asked to assist with the criminal case after the indictment in 2004. His total fees for work on behalf of Lay were approximately $640,000-$650,000 over four years. He testified without compensation at trial due to Lay's financial circumstances.

Barry's key testimony concerned:

  • Analysis of Lay's stock holdings demonstrating that approximately 90% of his liquid assets were Enron stock, indicating overconfidence rather than lack of belief in the company
  • Expert opinion that Lay's stock sales were forced by margin calls, not voluntary insider selling
  • Application of the Malmendier and Tate "CEO Overconfidence" research to argue Lay's holding patterns showed genuine belief in Enron
  • Demonstration that by November 2001, Lay's debt (~$8 million) nearly equaled his remaining collateral (~$15 million)

Much of Barry's testimony on CEO overconfidence was ultimately excluded by Judge Sim Lake under Rule 704B as inadmissible expert testimony on the mental state of a criminal defendant.

Robert Drayton McLane Jr. Character Witness — Defense
RM
TitleOwner, Houston Astros; Chairman, McLane Company
Called byDefense (Kenneth Lay)

Background

Robert Drayton McLane Jr. was born in Cameron, Texas and attended Baylor University and Michigan State University (M.A., 1959). He ran McLane Company, a family grocery distribution business founded by his grandfather in 1894, expanding it into a major distributor to supermarkets, convenience stores, and fast-food restaurants nationwide. The company merged with Wal-Mart in 1991. He purchased the Houston Astros in 1993.

Testimony

McLane testified as a character witness for Kenneth Lay. He described how he first came to know Lay through the effort to build a new baseball stadium in Houston. Rich Kinder, then Enron's president, introduced them. Lay personally helped raise $35 million in short-term funding for the stadium project and made over 30 civic speeches to help win the stadium referendum.

McLane testified that Lay was deeply involved in Houston philanthropy, including chairing the United Way (raising over $70 million) and building a new United Way headquarters ($25 million campaign). He described Lay as "very honorable" and someone whose word people would "take to the bank."

On cross-examination by prosecutor Cliff Stricklin, McLane acknowledged he did not work on the business side of Enron and had no knowledge of its corporate operations.

Role in the Case

Judge Sim Lake of the United States District Court for the Southern District of Texas, Houston Division, presided over the Enron criminal trial. The trial ran from January to May 2006 and was one of the most closely watched corporate fraud trials in American history.

During the trial, Judge Lake made several notable rulings visible in the transcripts, including sustaining the government's objection to defense expert Christopher Barry's testimony on CEO overconfidence under Rule 704B (prohibiting expert opinion on a defendant's mental state in criminal cases) and Rule 403 (marginal relevance outweighed by potential for confusion). He also managed discovery disputes regarding undisclosed expert slides and mediated contentious exchanges between counsel.
